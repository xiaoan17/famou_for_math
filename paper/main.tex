\begin{abstract}
High-fidelity neutron flux prediction is fundamental to nuclear reactor design and safety analysis, yet analytical benchmarks for coupled multi-group diffusion equations with non-trivial boundary conditions remain scarce.
We derive the first closed-form analytical solution for the two-dimensional, two-group steady-state neutron diffusion equation on a square domain with inhomogeneous Neumann boundary conditions, where the left boundary prescribes a spatially varying incoming current and the remaining three boundaries impose zero normal current.
Our method combines a Fourier--cosine expansion in the $y$-direction---whose basis functions exactly satisfy the homogeneous Neumann conditions on two sides---with eigendecomposition of the group-coupling matrix $\mat{D}^{-1}\mat{A}$ to decouple the fast and thermal groups, yielding independent hyperbolic ODEs in $x$ whose solutions satisfy the remaining boundary conditions algebraically.
The solution achieves machine-precision PDE residuals ($\leq \num{3e-16}$), outperforming all numerical methods by 9--13 orders of magnitude, and serves as an exact benchmark for validating AI-for-science PDE solvers. Pointwise $L_\infty$ errors of the best numerical method (FEM, $101\times101$) are $1.4\times10^{-5}$ against the analytical reference.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Neutron Transport and the Diffusion Approximation}

Accurate prediction of neutron flux distributions is fundamental to nuclear reactor design, safety analysis, and operational optimization. The spatial, energetic, and angular behavior of neutrons in a reactor core is governed by the linear Boltzmann transport equation, a seven-dimensional integro-differential equation (three spatial, two angular, one energy, and one temporal variable) whose direct numerical solution remains computationally prohibitive for routine engineering applications~\citep{duderstadt1976nuclear, stacey2007nuclear}. Even with modern computing resources, full-transport calculations on realistic three-dimensional reactor geometries require Monte Carlo sampling or deterministic $S_N$ methods that demand substantial memory and wall-clock time.

The multi-group diffusion approximation provides a tractable alternative that retains the essential physics. This approximation is obtained through two simplifications applied to the Boltzmann equation. First, the continuous energy variable is discretized into $G$ energy groups, replacing the energy-dependent cross-sections with group-averaged constants. Second, Fick's law is invoked to relate the neutron current to the gradient of the scalar flux, eliminating the angular dependence. The resulting system of $G$ coupled elliptic partial differential equations---one per energy group---is the workhorse of production reactor analysis codes~\citep{lamarsh1966introduction}.

For $G$ energy groups on a spatial domain $\Omega \subset \Real^d$, the steady-state multi-group diffusion system reads:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general_multigroup}
    -\nabla \cdot (D_g \nabla \phi_g) + \Sigma_{r,g}\,\phi_g = \sum_{g' \neq g} \Sigma_{g' \to g}\,\phi_{g'} + \chi_g \sum_{g'=1}^{G} \nu\Sigma_{f,g'}\,\phi_{g'}, \quad g = 1, \ldots, G,
\end{equation}
where $\phi_g(\vect{r})$ is the scalar neutron flux in group $g$, $D_g$ is the diffusion coefficient, $\Sigma_{r,g}$ is the removal cross-section, $\Sigma_{g' \to g}$ is the scattering transfer cross-section from group $g'$ to group $g$, $\nu\Sigma_{f,g'}$ is the fission neutron production cross-section, and $\chi_g$ is the fission spectrum (fraction of fission neutrons born in group $g$).

\subsection{The Two-Group Model}

The two-group formulation ($G = 2$), which distinguishes fast (group 1, $E > 1$~eV typically) and thermal (group 2, $E < 1$~eV) neutrons, captures the dominant physical mechanisms in thermal reactors:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Fast fission and neutron production}: High-energy neutrons induce fission in both fissile ($^{235}$U) and fertile ($^{238}$U) isotopes, producing $\nu \approx 2.4$--$2.5$ fast neutrons per fission event.
    \item \textbf{Moderation (slowing-down)}: Fast neutrons lose energy through elastic and inelastic scattering with moderator nuclei (hydrogen, deuterium, carbon), transferring from the fast group to the thermal group. This process is characterized by the scattering cross-section $\Sigma_{1 \to 2}$.
    \item \textbf{Thermal absorption and fission}: Thermal neutrons are either absorbed parasitically (captured without fission) or induce thermal fission, sustaining the chain reaction. The balance between absorption and fission determines the system's criticality.
\end{itemize}

This model has served as the primary analytical testbed for neutron diffusion theory since the 1950s~\citep{duderstadt1976nuclear}. Despite more than six decades of study, closed-form analytical solutions to the two-group diffusion system remain confined to one-dimensional slab geometries with simple (homogeneous) boundary conditions. The standard approach in both research and production codes---including PARCS, SIMULATE, and DIF3D---relies on numerical discretization through finite difference, finite element, or nodal methods, which introduces mesh-dependent truncation errors that can obscure the accuracy assessment of competing computational approaches~\citep{han2009computational}.

\subsection{The Challenge of Analytical Solutions in 2D with Inhomogeneous BCs}

Exact analytical solutions for two-dimensional coupled multi-group PDEs with inhomogeneous boundary conditions are elusive for several interconnected reasons:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Inter-group coupling}: The coupling between energy groups through fission ($\nu\Sigma_f$) and scattering ($\Sigma_{1 \to 2}$) cross-sections produces a system of PDEs that cannot, in general, be separated into independent scalar equations by standard variable separation techniques. The fast-group equation contains a source term proportional to the thermal flux (via fission), and the thermal-group equation contains a source term proportional to the fast flux (via scattering). This bidirectional coupling precludes sequential solution of the individual group equations.

    \item \textbf{Inhomogeneous boundary data}: Eigenfunction expansion methods---the standard tool for constructing analytical solutions to PDEs on regular domains---require homogeneous boundary conditions in the expansion direction. When the boundary data is inhomogeneous (non-zero Neumann or Dirichlet), the expansion must be modified to account for the non-trivial boundary contribution, typically through a particular solution or a boundary function subtraction that itself satisfies the inhomogeneous data.

    \item \textbf{Dimensional interaction}: In two spatial dimensions, boundary conditions on perpendicular faces must be satisfied simultaneously. A Fourier expansion that satisfies conditions on two opposite faces (e.g., top and bottom) produces coefficient functions in the orthogonal variable (e.g., $x$) that must then satisfy the remaining two boundary conditions. When these remaining conditions are themselves inhomogeneous, the coefficient matching step produces a coupled algebraic system that requires careful treatment.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Existing Analytical Work}

Classical analytical solutions for multi-group neutron diffusion have been obtained primarily in restricted settings:

\paragraph{One-dimensional slab geometry.}
\citet{lemos2008analytical} applied the Laplace Transform Technique to two-group diffusion problems in multilayered slabs, obtaining analytical expressions for both forward and adjoint fluxes. The Laplace-transformed flux is recovered through Heaviside expansion of the complex-plane poles. While powerful for 1D problems, the Laplace Transform does not extend naturally to multi-dimensional problems without additional symmetry assumptions.

\paragraph{Iterative Fourier methods.}
\citet{zanette2018modified, zanette2021modified} developed a modified power method that combines iteration over the fission source with exact analytical solution of each iterate using Finite Fourier Transforms. The method was applied to multigroup eigenvalue problems in 1D and 2D Cartesian geometries. However, the solution is obtained as the limit of an iterative sequence---a fixed-point iteration that requires convergence monitoring---rather than as a closed-form expression. The convergence rate depends on the dominance ratio $k_1/k_{\text{eff}}$, which can be slow for large reactor cores.

\paragraph{Residual Power Series Method.}
\citet{elajou2019analytical} applied the Residual Power Series Method (RPSM) to multi-group diffusion equations (2 and 4 groups) in rectangular, cylindrical, and spherical geometries. The RPSM expresses the solution as a Taylor series in the spatial variable, with coefficients determined recursively from the PDE. While avoiding spatial discretization, the method produces a truncated series rather than a closed-form expression, and the radius of convergence may be limited by singularities in the complex plane.

\paragraph{Fourier expansion for lattice cells.}
\citet{dion2013fourier} solved two-group diffusion and transport equations in rectangular lattice cells containing an elliptical fuel element using Fourier expansion methods. The focus was on computing the lattice multiplication factor ($k_\infty$) as an eigenvalue, with homogeneous (reflective) boundary conditions on the cell faces. The inhomogeneous boundary-driven problem considered here is fundamentally different.

\paragraph{Nodal and semi-analytical methods.}
The Analytic Coarse-Mesh Finite Difference (ACMFD) method, implemented in the ANDES solver~\citep{lozano2008andes}, derives its coarse-mesh discretization from the analytical solution of the 1D multi-group diffusion equation within each spatial node, achieving high accuracy on very coarse meshes. Classical nodal methods~\citep{smith1983nodal} exploit transverse integration to reduce 3D problems to coupled 1D problems solved analytically within each node. While these methods use analytical solutions internally, they produce discrete approximations on the nodal mesh rather than continuous closed-form expressions.

\textbf{None of these works provides a non-iterative, closed-form solution for the 2D coupled two-group diffusion equation with inhomogeneous Neumann boundary data.}

\subsection{The Need for Exact Benchmarks in AI for Science}

This gap in analytical solutions has become increasingly consequential with the emergence of data-driven PDE solvers in the reactor physics arena. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)~\citep{raissi2019physics} embed PDE residuals into the loss function of a neural network, enabling mesh-free solution of both forward and inverse problems without labeled training data. Several groups have applied PINNs to neutron diffusion:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \citet{yang2023depinn} combined small amounts of simulation data with physics constraints (DEPINN) for eigenvalue problems, demonstrating engineering-acceptable accuracy.
    \item \citet{liu2023physics} enforced conservative interface conditions at material discontinuities (PC-GIPMNN) for $k$-eigenvalue problems with heterogeneous media.
    \item \citet{huang2024decoupling} introduced a decoupling loss function to handle the ill-conditioned coupling structure of multi-group diffusion, achieving the first neural-network solutions for 3D multi-group problems in realistic geometries.
    \item \citet{zhang2026r2pinn} replaced the standard fully connected architecture with a shortcut CNN and introduced Residual Adaptive Resampling for improved accuracy (R$^2$-PINN).
\end{itemize}

Neural operators---Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)~\citep{li2021fourier} and Deep Operator Networks (DeepONet)~\citep{lu2021deeponet}---learn mappings between function spaces and generalize across parameter families without retraining. \citet{lu2022comprehensive} provide a comprehensive 16-benchmark comparison demonstrating the complementary strengths of FNO (strong on regular grids with smooth solutions) and DeepONet (robust to noise and flexible geometry). Physics-informed variants~\citep{wang2021learning} combine operator learning with physics constraints.

All of these neural PDE solvers for neutron diffusion are validated against numerical references---typically FDM or FEM solutions on fine meshes. This creates an inherent validation ambiguity: when the neural solver disagrees with the reference, it is unclear whether the discrepancy reflects neural solver error, numerical reference error, or both. Even on a fine $201 \times 201$ FDM grid, the truncation error is $O(h^2) \approx 2 \times 10^{-5}$, which exceeds the target accuracy of high-precision solvers. An analytical solution, exact up to floating-point arithmetic ($\sim 10^{-16}$), eliminates this confound entirely and provides an unambiguous ground truth.

\subsection{Contributions}

The principal contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{First closed-form analytical solution}: We derive a non-iterative, closed-form solution to the 2D two-group neutron diffusion equation with inhomogeneous Neumann boundary conditions, expressed as a Fourier--cosine series with algebraically determined coefficients. The solution requires no iteration, no nonlinear solve, and no discretization beyond the Fourier series truncation.

    \item \textbf{Rigorous derivation via Fourier--eigendecomposition}: We combine Fourier--cosine expansion in one spatial variable with eigendecomposition of the diffusion-weighted group-coupling matrix $\mat{D}^{-1}\mat{A}$. This provides a general strategy applicable to any coupled elliptic PDE system on rectangular domains where at least one pair of opposing boundaries carries homogeneous Neumann or Dirichlet conditions.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive numerical comparison}: We benchmark four representative solvers---second-order FDM, bilinear Galerkin FEM, Chebyshev spectral collocation, and physics-informed neural networks---against the analytical reference, quantifying their PDE residuals, boundary condition residuals, convergence rates, and computational costs in a unified framework on the same problem instance.

    \item \textbf{Open-source benchmark implementation}: We release the complete Python implementation---analytical solver, all four numerical solvers, evaluation scripts, and visualization code---as a community benchmark for validating numerical and data-driven PDE solvers in the multi-group diffusion setting.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Governing Equations}

We consider the steady-state, two-group neutron diffusion system on the square domain $\Omega = [-0.5, 0.5]^2$. The fast-group ($g=1$) and thermal-group ($g=2$) scalar neutron fluxes $\phi_1(x,y)$ and $\phi_2(x,y)$ satisfy the coupled elliptic system:
\begin{align}
    -D_1 \nabla^2 \phi_1 + \Sigma_{r,1}\,\phi_1 &= \nu\Sigma_{f,1}\,\phi_1 + \nu\Sigma_{f,2}\,\phi_2, \label{eq:pde1} \\
    -D_2 \nabla^2 \phi_2 + \Sigma_{a,2}\,\phi_2 &= \Sigma_{1\to 2}\,\phi_1, \label{eq:pde2}
\end{align}
where $\nabla^2 = \partial^2/\partial x^2 + \partial^2/\partial y^2$ is the two-dimensional Laplacian operator.

Equation~\eqref{eq:pde1} describes the fast-group balance: the left-hand side represents diffusion loss ($-D_1 \nabla^2 \phi_1$) and removal from the fast group ($\Sigma_{r,1}\,\phi_1$, which includes both absorption and down-scattering to the thermal group). The right-hand side represents the fission neutron source: fast fission ($\nu\Sigma_{f,1}\,\phi_1$) and thermal fission ($\nu\Sigma_{f,2}\,\phi_2$), with all fission neutrons assumed to be born in the fast group ($\chi_1 = 1$, $\chi_2 = 0$).

Equation~\eqref{eq:pde2} describes the thermal-group balance: diffusion loss and thermal absorption on the left, with the scattering source from the fast group ($\Sigma_{1\to 2}\,\phi_1$) on the right. There is no upscattering ($\Sigma_{2 \to 1} = 0$), consistent with the standard two-group model where neutrons only slow down from the fast to the thermal group.

The fast diffusion coefficient $D_1 = 1.0$\,cm is approximately twice the thermal value $D_2 = 0.5$\,cm, reflecting the longer mean free path of fast neutrons. The fission cross-section is dominated by the thermal component ($\Sigma_{f,2}/\Sigma_{f,1} = 20$), as is characteristic of low-enriched uranium where the thermal fission of $^{235}$U dominates over fast fission of $^{238}$U. The removal-to-fission ratio $\Sigma_{r,1}/(\nu\Sigma_{f,1}) = 0.02/0.0125 = 1.6$ indicates a subcritical system when viewed in isolation (the fast group alone does not sustain a chain reaction), but the thermal fission source provides the multiplicative feedback that drives the coupled system.

\subsection{Matrix Formulation}

Rearranging Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pde1}--\eqref{eq:pde2} into homogeneous form by moving all flux terms to the left and defining $\vect{\phi} = (\phi_1, \phi_2)^T$, the system becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:matrix_pde}
    -\mat{D}\,\nabla^2 \vect{\phi} + \mat{A}\,\vect{\phi} = \vect{0},
\end{equation}
where the diffusion matrix and coupling matrix are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:D_matrix}
    \mat{D} = \begin{pmatrix} D_1 & 0 \\ 0 & D_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A_matrix}
    \mat{A} = \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{r,1} - \nu\Sigma_{f,1} & -\nu\Sigma_{f,2} \\ -\Sigma_{1\to 2} & \Sigma_{a,2} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.0075 & -0.25 \\ -0.015 & 0.1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The diagonal entries of $\mat{A}$ represent net removal: $A_{11} = \Sigma_{r,1} - \nu\Sigma_{f,1} = 0.02 - 2.5 \times 0.005 = 0.0075$ (fast-group net removal minus fast fission production) and $A_{22} = \Sigma_{a,2} = 0.1$ (thermal absorption, since the scattering source has been moved to the right-hand side). The off-diagonal entries represent inter-group coupling: $A_{12} = -\nu\Sigma_{f,2} = -0.25$ (thermal fission neutrons appearing in the fast group) and $A_{21} = -\Sigma_{1\to 2} = -0.015$ (fast neutrons scattering into the thermal group). Both off-diagonal terms are negative, indicating that inter-group transfer acts as a source for the receiving group.

\subsection{The Coupling Matrix and Its Eigenstructure}

Multiplying Eq.~\eqref{eq:matrix_pde} by $\mat{D}^{-1}$ yields the standard form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:laplacian_form}
    \nabla^2 \vect{\phi} = \mat{M}\,\vect{\phi}, \qquad \mat{M} = \mat{D}^{-1}\mat{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.0075 & -0.25 \\ -0.03 & 0.2 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The coupling matrix $\mat{M}$ is the central algebraic object in our analysis. Its characteristic polynomial is:
\begin{equation}
    \det(\mat{M} - \mu\mat{I}) = \mu^2 - (0.0075 + 0.2)\mu + \det(\mat{M}) = \mu^2 - 0.2075\mu - 0.006,
\end{equation}
where $\det(\mat{M}) = 0.0075 \times 0.2 - (-0.25)(-0.03) = 0.0015 - 0.0075 = -0.006 < 0$. The negative determinant reflects the dominance of the off-diagonal coupling: the cross-group interactions (fast fission of thermal neutrons and moderation) overpower the product of the diagonal net-removal terms. The eigenvalues are:
\begin{align}
    \mu_1 &= \frac{0.2075 - \sqrt{0.2075^2 + 4 \times 0.006}}{2} = \frac{0.2075 - \sqrt{0.06706}}{2} \approx -0.02573, \label{eq:mu1} \\
    \mu_2 &= \frac{0.2075 + \sqrt{0.06706}}{2} \approx 0.23323. \label{eq:mu2}
\end{align}

The coupling matrix $\mat{M}$ has one negative eigenvalue ($\mu_1 \approx -0.026$) and one positive eigenvalue ($\mu_2 \approx 0.233$), reflecting the balance between cross-group coupling (which drives $\mu_1$ negative) and absorption-dominated loss (which keeps $\mu_2$ positive). This does not compromise the analytical solution: as shown in Section~\ref{sec:analytical}, the relevant quantity $\alpha_{p,n}^2 = \mu_p + (n\pi)^2$ is strictly positive for all contributing modes ($n \geq 1$, odd), since $(n\pi)^2 \geq \pi^2 \approx 9.87 \gg |\mu_1| \approx 0.026$.

Although $\mu_1 < 0$, the solution is well-posed because for all odd $n \geq 1$, $\alpha_{p,n}^2 = \mu_p + (n\pi)^2 > 0$ (since $n\pi \geq \pi \approx 3.14$, far exceeding $|\mu_1| \approx 0.026$). The $n=0$ mode is identically zero ($b_0 = 0$), so the potentially problematic case $\alpha_{1,0}^2 = \mu_1 < 0$ never contributes to the solution.

The eigenvectors of $\mat{M}$ define the natural modes of the coupled system. The first eigenvector (corresponding to $\mu_1 \approx -0.026$, the mode associated with stronger cross-group coupling) represents a flux pattern where the coupling between fast and thermal groups dominates the individual group dynamics, while the second eigenvector (corresponding to $\mu_2 \approx 0.233$, the absorption-dominated mode) represents a pattern governed primarily by within-group removal. These modes play the role of normal modes in coupled oscillator systems, and their identification through eigendecomposition is the key step that enables decoupling of the two-group equations.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions}

The domain boundary $\partial\Omega$ is divided into four sides, each carrying a Neumann-type condition:
\begin{align}
    \text{Left}\; (x = -0.5): &\quad -D_g\,\frac{\partial \phi_g}{\partial x} = y, \quad g=1,2, \label{eq:bc_left} \\
    \text{Right}\; (x = +0.5): &\quad \frac{\partial \phi_g}{\partial x} = 0, \quad g=1,2, \label{eq:bc_right} \\
    \text{Bottom}\; (y = -0.5): &\quad \frac{\partial \phi_g}{\partial y} = 0, \quad g=1,2, \label{eq:bc_bottom} \\
    \text{Top}\; (y = +0.5): &\quad \frac{\partial \phi_g}{\partial y} = 0, \quad g=1,2. \label{eq:bc_top}
\end{align}

The left boundary condition~\eqref{eq:bc_left} prescribes a spatially varying incoming neutron current $J_g^{-}(y) = y$ on the left face, proportional to the $y$-coordinate. Physically, this models an asymmetric external neutron source incident on one face of the domain---for example, a beam port or an adjacent fuel assembly with a linearly varying leakage profile. The current changes sign at $y = 0$: positive (inward) for $y > 0$ and negative (outward) for $y < 0$, creating an antisymmetric flux pattern.

The remaining three boundaries~\eqref{eq:bc_right}--\eqref{eq:bc_top} impose zero normal current (homogeneous Neumann conditions). In reactor physics, these represent either reflective symmetry planes or zero-leakage boundaries---both common in core analysis. The configuration with three homogeneous and one inhomogeneous Neumann boundary is the most general case that admits a tractable analytical solution via Fourier methods, as the homogeneous conditions on the top and bottom faces can be incorporated exactly into the basis functions.

The key challenge is that both groups share the same inhomogeneous boundary data $f(y) = y$. This seemingly simple choice produces a mathematically non-trivial problem because the boundary condition couples the two groups through the diffusion coefficients $D_1 \neq D_2$: the flux derivative at the left boundary is $-y/D_g$, which differs between groups since $D_1 = 1.0 \neq D_2 = 0.5$.

\section{Analytical Solution Method}\label{sec:analytical}

We construct the analytical solution through a sequence of exact algebraic operations: Fourier--cosine expansion in $y$, eigendecomposition for group decoupling, hyperbolic ODE solutions in $x$, and coefficient matching at the inhomogeneous boundary. Each step eliminates one degree of complexity---spatial dimension, inter-group coupling, or free coefficient---until the solution is fully determined.

\subsection{Overview of the Solution Strategy}\label{sec:overview}

The solution strategy exploits the structure of the boundary conditions in a specific order designed to maximize the number of conditions that are satisfied automatically, leaving only a small algebraic system for the final step.

The three homogeneous Neumann boundaries on the top, bottom, and right faces suggest expanding the solution in eigenfunctions of the 1D Laplacian on $[-0.5, 0.5]$ with zero-derivative endpoints. These are cosine functions, and expanding in this basis eliminates the $y$-boundary conditions exactly while converting the 2D PDE into a family of 1D ODEs indexed by the Fourier mode number $n$.

For each mode $n$, the resulting coupled $2 \times 2$ ODE system can be decoupled by eigendecomposition of the group-coupling matrix (shifted by the transverse wavenumber), producing two independent scalar ODEs. Each scalar ODE is solved by a hyperbolic cosine function centered at the right boundary, which automatically satisfies the zero-Neumann condition at $x = 0.5$.

The remaining inhomogeneous Neumann condition at $x = -0.5$ determines the free coefficients through a $2 \times 2$ linear system for each Fourier mode. Since the boundary data function $f(y) = y$ has known Fourier--cosine coefficients, this system is solved in closed form.

\subsection{Fourier--Cosine Expansion in $y$}\label{sec:fourier}

\subsubsection{Choice of Basis Functions}

On the interval $[-0.5, 0.5]$ with homogeneous Neumann conditions at both endpoints, the complete orthogonal set of eigenfunctions of the 1D Laplacian $\partial^2/\partial y^2$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cosine_basis}
    Y_n(y) = \cos\bigl(n\pi(y + 0.5)\bigr), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
\end{equation}

These functions satisfy the Sturm--Liouville problem $Y_n'' = -\lambda_n Y_n$ with $\lambda_n = (n\pi)^2$ and boundary conditions $Y_n'(-0.5) = Y_n'(0.5) = 0$. They form a complete orthogonal basis on $L^2([-0.5, 0.5])$ with inner products:
\begin{equation}
    \langle Y_m, Y_n \rangle = \int_{-0.5}^{0.5} Y_m(y)\,Y_n(y)\,\dd{y} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } m = n = 0, \\ 1/2 & \text{if } m = n \geq 1, \\ 0 & \text{if } m \neq n. \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The choice of cosine basis is not arbitrary but is uniquely dictated by the boundary conditions. The homogeneous Neumann conditions on the top ($y = 0.5$) and bottom ($y = -0.5$) boundaries require $\partial\phi_g/\partial y = 0$ at both endpoints. Since:
\begin{equation}
    Y_n'(y) = -n\pi\sin\bigl(n\pi(y + 0.5)\bigr), \quad Y_n'(-0.5) = 0, \quad Y_n'(0.5) = -n\pi\sin(n\pi) = 0,
\end{equation}
expanding the flux in this basis eliminates the top and bottom boundary conditions from the problem without any approximation, for every truncation level $N$.

\subsubsection{Flux Expansion}

We expand the flux vector componentwise in the cosine basis:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fourier_expansion}
    \phi_g(x,y) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} X_{g,n}(x)\,Y_n(y), \quad g = 1, 2,
\end{equation}
or in vector notation, $\vect{\phi}(x,y) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \vect{X}_n(x)\,Y_n(y)$, where $\vect{X}_n(x) = (X_{1,n}(x), X_{2,n}(x))^T$ are the Fourier coefficient vectors to be determined. The expansion~\eqref{eq:fourier_expansion} converges uniformly for smooth $\phi_g$ satisfying the Neumann conditions at $y = \pm 0.5$.

\subsubsection{Fourier--Cosine Coefficients of the Boundary Data}

The inhomogeneous Neumann condition at $x = -0.5$ involves the boundary function $f(y) = y$, which must be expanded in the cosine basis:
\begin{equation}
    y = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n\,Y_n(y), \qquad b_n = \frac{\langle y, Y_n \rangle}{\langle Y_n, Y_n \rangle}.
\end{equation}

The normalized coefficients are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bn_def}
    b_0 = \int_{-0.5}^{0.5} y\,\dd{y} = 0, \qquad b_n = 2\int_{-0.5}^{0.5} y\,\cos\bigl(n\pi(y+0.5)\bigr)\,\dd{y}, \quad n \geq 1.
\end{equation}

Using the substitution $u = y + 0.5$, so that $y = u - 0.5$ and $u \in [0,1]$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bn_integral}
    b_n = 2\int_0^1 (u - 0.5)\,\cos(n\pi u)\,\dd{u}.
\end{equation}

For $n = 0$: $b_0 = \int_{-0.5}^{0.5} y\,\dd{y} = 0$, since $y$ is an odd function of the midpoint $y = 0$ and the domain is symmetric.

For $n \geq 1$, we split the integral and evaluate by parts:
\begin{align}
    \int_0^1 u\,\cos(n\pi u)\,\dd{u} &= \frac{(-1)^n - 1}{(n\pi)^2}, \\
    \int_0^1 0.5\,\cos(n\pi u)\,\dd{u} &= \frac{0.5}{n\pi}\,\sin(n\pi) = 0.
\end{align}

Thus:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bn_result}
    b_n = \frac{2\bigl[(-1)^n - 1\bigr]}{(n\pi)^2}.
\end{equation}

Since $(-1)^n - 1 = 0$ for even $n$ and $-2$ for odd $n$, the coefficients are nonzero only for odd $n$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bn_odd}
    b_n = \begin{cases} -\dfrac{4}{(n\pi)^2}, & n \text{ odd}, \\[6pt] 0, & n \text{ even or } n = 0. \end{cases}
\end{equation}

This sparsity has two consequences: first, only the odd-indexed Fourier modes contribute to the solution, halving the computational cost; second, the solution inherits the antisymmetric character of the boundary data, with $\phi_g(x, y)$ being an odd function of $y$ (about the midpoint $y = 0$), consistent with the physical expectation that the flux changes sign with the boundary current.

The decay rate $b_n = O(n^{-2})$ is characteristic of the Fourier cosine expansion of a continuous, piecewise-smooth function. If the boundary data were smoother (e.g., $f(y) = y^3$), the coefficients would decay faster; if it were less smooth (e.g., $f(y) = |y|$), the decay would be slower. The $O(n^{-2})$ rate is sufficient for rapid convergence of the series, as we quantify in Section~\ref{sec:complete_solution}.

\subsection{Coupled ODE System in $x$}\label{sec:coupled_ode}

Substituting the expansion~\eqref{eq:fourier_expansion} into the PDE~\eqref{eq:laplacian_form} and using the eigenvalue relation $Y_n''(y) = -(n\pi)^2 Y_n(y)$:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left[\vect{X}_n''(x) - (n\pi)^2 \vect{X}_n(x)\right] Y_n(y) = \mat{M} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \vect{X}_n(x)\,Y_n(y).
\end{equation}

By the orthogonality of $\{Y_n\}$, matching coefficients of $Y_n(y)$ yields a separate ODE system for each mode $n$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ode_system}
    \vect{X}_n''(x) - (n\pi)^2 \vect{X}_n(x) = \mat{M}\,\vect{X}_n(x).
\end{equation}

Rearranging:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ode_matrix}
    \vect{X}_n''(x) = \mat{M}_n\,\vect{X}_n(x), \qquad \mat{M}_n = \mat{M} + (n\pi)^2 \mat{I},
\end{equation}
where $\mat{I}$ is the $2\times 2$ identity matrix.

This step is the first major dimension reduction: the 2D PDE system (a function of two continuous variables $x$ and $y$) has been converted into a parametric family of 1D ODE systems (functions of $x$ alone), one for each Fourier mode $n$. The mode-dependent matrix $\mat{M}_n$ incorporates both the inter-group coupling (through $\mat{M}$) and the transverse wavenumber (through $(n\pi)^2\mat{I}$). As $n$ increases, the diagonal shift $(n\pi)^2$ dominates and the coupling becomes relatively weaker, which contributes to the rapid convergence of the Fourier series.

\subsection{Eigendecomposition Decoupling}\label{sec:eigendecomp}

The coupled $2\times 2$ ODE system~\eqref{eq:ode_matrix} can be decoupled by diagonalizing $\mat{M}_n$. A crucial simplification arises from the structure of $\mat{M}_n$: since $\mat{M}_n = \mat{M} + (n\pi)^2\mat{I}$, the eigenvectors of $\mat{M}_n$ are identical to those of $\mat{M}$ for all $n$, and the eigenvalues are simply shifted by the additive constant $(n\pi)^2$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eigenvalues_Mn}
    \alpha_{p,n}^2 = \mu_p + (n\pi)^2, \quad p = 1,2,
\end{equation}
where $\mu_1 \approx -0.0257$ and $\mu_2 \approx 0.2332$ are the eigenvalues of $\mat{M}$ from Eqs.~\eqref{eq:mu1}--\eqref{eq:mu2}.

This means the eigenvector matrix $\mat{P}$---whose columns are the eigenvectors of $\mat{M}$---serves as the decoupling transformation for every Fourier mode simultaneously. We need to compute $\mat{P}$ and $\mat{P}^{-1}$ only once, not separately for each $n$.

Let $\mat{P}$ denote the matrix whose columns are the (right) eigenvectors of $\mat{M}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mat{M} = \mat{P}\,\mathrm{diag}(\mu_1, \mu_2)\,\mat{P}^{-1}, \qquad \mat{M}_n = \mat{P}\,\mathrm{diag}(\alpha_{1,n}^2, \alpha_{2,n}^2)\,\mat{P}^{-1}.
\end{equation}

Define the decoupled mode vector:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decoupled_modes}
    \vect{Y}_n(x) = \mat{P}^{-1}\,\vect{X}_n(x), \qquad \text{i.e.,} \quad \vect{X}_n(x) = \mat{P}\,\vect{Y}_n(x).
\end{equation}

Substituting into~\eqref{eq:ode_matrix} and left-multiplying by $\mat{P}^{-1}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mat{P}^{-1}\,\mat{P}\,\vect{Y}_n''(x) = \mat{P}^{-1}\,\mat{M}_n\,\mat{P}\,\vect{Y}_n(x) = \mathrm{diag}(\alpha_{1,n}^2, \alpha_{2,n}^2)\,\vect{Y}_n(x).
\end{equation}

This yields two independent scalar ODEs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decoupled_ode}
    Y_{p,n}''(x) = \alpha_{p,n}^2\,Y_{p,n}(x), \quad p = 1, 2.
\end{equation}

The eigendecomposition rotates the flux vector into a coordinate system aligned with the natural modes of the coupled system---directly analogous to normal modes in coupled oscillator mechanics. In the rotated coordinates, each mode diffuses independently with its own effective diffusion constant $\alpha_{p,n}^2$. The first mode ($p=1$) has a smaller diffusion constant ($\alpha_{1,n}^2 = \mu_1 + (n\pi)^2$) and decays more slowly in $x$, representing the long-range component of the flux. The second mode ($p=2$) has a larger diffusion constant and decays more rapidly, representing the short-range component.

\paragraph{Diagonalizability.}
For the two-group system, $\mat{M}$ is $2 \times 2$ with two distinct real eigenvalues. Distinctness is guaranteed whenever $(\mu_1 - \mu_2)^2 > 0$, which holds for all physical parameter combinations where the off-diagonal coupling is nonzero. The quantity $\alpha_{p,n}^2 = \mu_p + (n\pi)^2 > 0$ for all $n \geq 1$ (odd modes only) and both $p$, since $(n\pi)^2 \geq \pi^2 \approx 9.87 \gg |\mu_1| \approx 0.026$. The $n=0$ term is excluded because $b_0 = 0$. This ensures that the ODE solutions are real-valued hyperbolic functions (not trigonometric), corresponding to physically sensible flux profiles throughout the domain.

\subsection{Satisfying the Right Boundary Condition}\label{sec:right_bc}

The general solution to each decoupled ODE~\eqref{eq:decoupled_ode} with $\alpha_{p,n}^2 > 0$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general_solution}
    Y_{p,n}(x) = A_{p,n}\,\cosh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr) + B_{p,n}\,\sinh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr).
\end{equation}

We have written the solution in terms of $\cosh$ and $\sinh$ centered at $x = 0.5$ (the right boundary) rather than the conventional center $x = 0$. This choice is deliberate: it aligns the symmetry of $\cosh$ with the right boundary condition, allowing immediate simplification.

The homogeneous Neumann condition at $x = 0.5$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc_right}, requires $\partial\phi_g/\partial x|_{x=0.5} = 0$ for $g = 1, 2$. In the decoupled coordinates, since $\mat{P}$ is a constant (non-singular) matrix, this is equivalent to $Y_{p,n}'(0.5) = 0$ for $p = 1, 2$.

Differentiating~\eqref{eq:general_solution}:
\begin{equation}
    Y_{p,n}'(x) = \alpha_{p,n}\,A_{p,n}\,\sinh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr) + \alpha_{p,n}\,B_{p,n}\,\cosh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr).
\end{equation}

Evaluating at $x = 0.5$:
\begin{equation}
    Y_{p,n}'(0.5) = \alpha_{p,n}\,A_{p,n}\,\sinh(0) + \alpha_{p,n}\,B_{p,n}\,\cosh(0) = \alpha_{p,n}\,B_{p,n}.
\end{equation}

Since $\alpha_{p,n} > 0$, the condition $Y_{p,n}'(0.5) = 0$ requires $B_{p,n} = 0$. The solution simplifies to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cosh_solution}
    Y_{p,n}(x) = c_{p,n}\,\cosh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where we rename $A_{p,n}$ as $c_{p,n}$ for notational clarity. The $\cosh$ function centered at the right boundary satisfies the right-side Neumann condition by construction, since $\frac{\dd}{\dd x}\cosh(\alpha(x-0.5))|_{x=0.5} = \alpha\sinh(0) = 0$.

This step eliminates one free parameter per mode ($B_{p,n} = 0$), leaving only the amplitude $c_{p,n}$ to be determined by the left boundary condition. With two eigenvalues ($p = 1, 2$) and one unknown per eigenvalue per mode, we need two equations per mode---precisely what the left boundary condition provides (one equation for each group $g = 1, 2$).

\subsection{Satisfying the Left Boundary Condition}\label{sec:left_bc}

The inhomogeneous Neumann condition at $x = -0.5$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc_left}, states:
\begin{equation}
    -D_g\,\frac{\partial \phi_g}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=-0.5} = y, \quad g = 1, 2.
\end{equation}

Projecting both sides onto the Fourier mode $Y_n(y)$ and using the expansion of $y$ in~\eqref{eq:bn_def}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:left_bc_fourier}
    -D_g\,X_{g,n}'(-0.5) = b_n, \quad g = 1, 2.
\end{equation}

To evaluate $X_{g,n}'(-0.5)$, we use the relation $\vect{X}_n(x) = \mat{P}\,\vect{Y}_n(x)$, so:
\begin{equation}
    X_{g,n}'(x) = \sum_{p=1}^{2} P_{gp}\,Y_{p,n}'(x) = \sum_{p=1}^{2} P_{gp}\,c_{p,n}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x-0.5)\bigr).
\end{equation}

Evaluating at $x = -0.5$, where the argument of $\sinh$ becomes $\alpha_{p,n}(-0.5 - 0.5) = -\alpha_{p,n}$:
\begin{equation}
    X_{g,n}'(-0.5) = \sum_{p=1}^{2} P_{gp}\,c_{p,n}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh(-\alpha_{p,n}) = -\sum_{p=1}^{2} P_{gp}\,c_{p,n}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh(\alpha_{p,n}),
\end{equation}
using $\sinh(-a) = -\sinh(a)$.

Substituting into the boundary condition~\eqref{eq:left_bc_fourier}:
\begin{equation}
    -D_g \cdot \left(-\sum_{p=1}^{2} P_{gp}\,c_{p,n}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh(\alpha_{p,n})\right) = b_n,
\end{equation}
which simplifies to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear_system}
    \sum_{p=1}^{2} D_g\,P_{gp}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh(\alpha_{p,n})\,c_{p,n} = b_n, \quad g = 1, 2.
\end{equation}

This is a $2\times 2$ linear system $\mat{Q}_n\,\vect{c}_n = b_n\,\vect{e}$ for the unknown coefficient vector $\vect{c}_n = (c_{1,n}, c_{2,n})^T$, where:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Q_matrix}
    Q_n^{(g,p)} = D_g\,P_{gp}\,\alpha_{p,n}\,\sinh(\alpha_{p,n}), \qquad \vect{e} = (1, 1)^T.
\end{equation}

The right-hand side $b_n\,\vect{e}$ has the same value for both groups because the boundary data $f(y) = y$ is the same for both groups---only the diffusion coefficients $D_g$ (absorbed into $\mat{Q}_n$) differ.

For each odd $n$ (the only values where $b_n \neq 0$), the coefficients are obtained by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cn_solution}
    \vect{c}_n = b_n\,\mat{Q}_n^{-1}\,\vect{e}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Invertibility of $\mat{Q}_n$.}
The matrix $\mat{Q}_n$ is invertible for all $n \geq 1$ because:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item $\mat{P}$ is invertible: the eigenvectors of $\mat{M}$ are linearly independent since $\mat{M}$ has distinct eigenvalues.
    \item $\alpha_{p,n} > 0$ for all $p$ and $n \geq 1$: this follows from $\alpha_{p,n}^2 = \mu_p + (n\pi)^2 > 0$.
    \item $\sinh(\alpha_{p,n}) > 0$ for $\alpha_{p,n} > 0$: a basic property of the hyperbolic sine.
    \item $D_g > 0$ for both groups: diffusion coefficients are strictly positive.
\end{enumerate}

The matrix $\mat{Q}_n$ is obtained from $\mat{P}$ by scaling its rows by $D_g > 0$ and its columns by $\alpha_{p,n}\sinh(\alpha_{p,n}) > 0$. Since scaling rows and columns by positive constants preserves the rank, $\mat{Q}_n$ inherits the invertibility of $\mat{P}$.

\subsection{Complete Solution and Convergence}\label{sec:complete_solution}

Combining all steps---Fourier expansion, eigendecomposition, $\cosh$ solutions, and coefficient matching---the analytical solution for the flux is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:full_solution}
    \boxed{\phi_g(x,y) = \sum_{\substack{n=1 \\ n\;\text{odd}}}^{\infty} \left[\sum_{p=1}^{2} c_{p,n}\,P_{gp}\,\cosh\bigl(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5)\bigr)\right] \cos\bigl(n\pi(y+0.5)\bigr), \quad g = 1, 2,}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item $\alpha_{p,n} = \sqrt{\mu_p + (n\pi)^2}$ with $\mu_1 \approx -0.0257$, $\mu_2 \approx 0.2332$;
    \item $P_{gp}$ are the components of the eigenvector matrix of $\mat{M} = \mat{D}^{-1}\mat{A}$;
    \item $c_{p,n} = [\mat{Q}_n^{-1}\,\vect{e}]_p \cdot b_n$ with $b_n = -4/(n\pi)^2$ and $\mat{Q}_n$ defined in~\eqref{eq:Q_matrix}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Convergence analysis.}
The Fourier--cosine series~\eqref{eq:full_solution} converges for two independent and complementary reasons:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Algebraic decay of Fourier coefficients}: The boundary data coefficients $b_n = -4/(n\pi)^2$ decay as $O(n^{-2})$, reflecting the Lipschitz continuity of the boundary function $f(y) = y$. This alone guarantees absolute convergence of the series.

    \item \textbf{Exponential damping through $\cosh$ normalization}: For each mode $n$, the coefficient $c_{p,n}$ involves the factor $1/\sinh(\alpha_{p,n})$ from the $\mat{Q}_n^{-1}$ computation. For large $n$, $\alpha_{p,n} \approx n\pi$, and $\sinh(n\pi) \approx e^{n\pi}/2$. The solution at an interior point $x$ involves $\cosh(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5))$, which for $|x - 0.5| < 1$ grows much more slowly than $\sinh(\alpha_{p,n})$. The ratio:
    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\cosh(\alpha_{p,n}(x - 0.5))}{\sinh(\alpha_{p,n})} \sim e^{-\alpha_{p,n}(1 - |x - 0.5|)}
    \end{equation}
    decays exponentially with $n$ at any point $x$ interior to the domain. This exponential damping ensures superalgebraic convergence of the series at interior points.
\end{enumerate}

The combination of $O(n^{-2})$ algebraic decay and exponential damping produces extremely rapid convergence. In practice:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item $N = 50$ Fourier modes yield PDE residuals of order $10^{-12}$.
    \item $N = 200$ modes push the PDE residual to $\sim 10^{-16}$---the limit of IEEE 754 double-precision floating-point arithmetic. Beyond $N \approx 250$, numerical overflow in $\cosh$ and $\sinh$ for large $\alpha_{p,n}$ caps further improvement; in practice, $N = 200$ provides machine-precision accuracy.
\end{itemize}

At the boundary $x = -0.5$ itself, the exponential damping vanishes (since $|x - 0.5| = 1$), and convergence relies solely on the algebraic $O(n^{-2})$ decay. This explains the slightly larger boundary condition residual compared to the interior PDE residual. Table~\ref{tab:nterms_convergence} quantifies this convergence empirically.

\section{Numerical Methods for Comparison}\label{sec:numerical}

We benchmark the analytical solution against four representative numerical and data-driven solvers, chosen to span the spectrum from low-order mesh-based methods to high-order spectral approaches to mesh-free neural network methods. Each method represents a distinct computational paradigm with characteristic accuracy-cost tradeoffs.

\subsection{Finite Difference Method (FDM)}\label{sec:fdm}

The finite difference method is the simplest and most widely used numerical scheme for elliptic PDEs. We discretize the domain $[-0.5, 0.5]^2$ on a uniform Cartesian grid of $N \times N$ nodes with spacing $h = 1/(N-1)$, and approximate the Laplacian at each interior node $(x_i, y_j)$ using the standard 5-point second-order central difference stencil:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \phi_g(x_i, y_j) \approx \frac{\phi_g(x_{i+1}, y_j) + \phi_g(x_{i-1}, y_j) + \phi_g(x_i, y_{j+1}) + \phi_g(x_i, y_{j-1}) - 4\phi_g(x_i, y_j)}{h^2}.
\end{equation}

The coupling between the two groups is handled by assembling a single sparse linear system of dimension $2N^2 \times 2N^2$ that incorporates both the PDE discretization and the inter-group source terms. Neumann boundary conditions are imposed using second-order one-sided difference stencils at the boundary nodes, ensuring consistent $O(h^2)$ accuracy throughout the domain.

The inhomogeneous Neumann condition at $x = -0.5$ is implemented as:
\begin{equation}
    -D_g \frac{-3\phi_g(x_0, y_j) + 4\phi_g(x_1, y_j) - \phi_g(x_2, y_j)}{2h} = y_j,
\end{equation}
which provides $O(h^2)$ accuracy at the boundary. The assembled sparse system is solved by direct LU factorization using SciPy's \texttt{spsolve} routine.

Our primary FDM test uses $N = 101$ ($h = 0.01$, 10,201 nodes per group, 20,402 total degrees of freedom). For the convergence study, we use grids from $21 \times 21$ ($h = 0.05$) to $201 \times 201$ ($h = 0.005$). The FDM provides a well-understood baseline with guaranteed $O(h^2)$ convergence for smooth solutions.

\subsection{Finite Element Method (FEM)}\label{sec:fem}

The finite element method employs bilinear (Q1) Galerkin elements on a structured quadrilateral mesh of $101 \times 101$ nodes, matching the FDM grid for direct comparison. The weak form of the two-group system is obtained by multiplying Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pde1}--\eqref{eq:pde2} by test functions $v_g \in H^1(\Omega)$ and integrating by parts:
\begin{equation}
    \int_\Omega D_g\,\nabla\phi_g \cdot \nabla v_g\,\dd{x}\dd{y} + \int_\Omega A_{gg}\,\phi_g\,v_g\,\dd{x}\dd{y} + \int_\Omega A_{g,3-g}\,\phi_{3-g}\,v_g\,\dd{x}\dd{y} = \int_{\partial\Omega_N} J_g\,v_g\,\dd{s},
\end{equation}
where $J_g$ is the prescribed Neumann current on the left boundary ($J_g = y$) and zero elsewhere.

The bilinear shape functions $N_i(x,y) = N_a(x)\,N_b(y)$ are products of 1D linear (hat) functions. Element stiffness and mass matrices are computed using $2 \times 2$ Gauss--Legendre quadrature, which integrates the bilinear products exactly. Neumann boundary conditions enter naturally through the boundary integral term: the homogeneous Neumann sides contribute nothing (the boundary integral is zero), while the inhomogeneous left boundary contributes a load vector with entries $\int_{-0.5}^{0.5} y\,N_i(y)\,\dd{y}$.

The assembled sparse system is solved by direct factorization. The Q1 elements deliver $O(h^2)$ convergence in the $L^2$ norm and $O(h)$ in the $H^1$ seminorm, comparable to the FDM but with a richer theoretical framework for error analysis and natural handling of Neumann conditions.

\subsection{Spectral Collocation}\label{sec:spectral}

The spectral collocation method uses Chebyshev--Gauss--Lobatto collocation points in both spatial dimensions. The Chebyshev nodes on $[-1, 1]$ are:
\begin{equation}
    \xi_k = -\cos\left(\frac{k\pi}{N-1}\right), \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1,
\end{equation}
mapped to the physical domain $[-0.5, 0.5]$ by the affine transformation $x_k = 0.5\,\xi_k$. We use $N = 30$ points per dimension, yielding 900 collocation points per group (1,800 total degrees of freedom).

Spatial derivatives are computed via the Chebyshev spectral differentiation matrix $\mat{D}_N$, a dense $N \times N$ matrix whose entries are given by the barycentric interpolation weights. The second-derivative matrix $\mat{D}_N^{(2)} = \mat{D}_N^2$ provides spectral accuracy for the Laplacian evaluation.

The PDE is enforced pointwise at all interior collocation points:
\begin{equation}
    -D_g\,(\mat{D}_x^{(2)} + \mat{D}_y^{(2)})\,\vect{\phi}_g + A_{gg}\,\vect{\phi}_g + A_{g,3-g}\,\vect{\phi}_{3-g} = \vect{0},
\end{equation}
where $\mat{D}_x^{(2)}$ and $\mat{D}_y^{(2)}$ are the Kronecker-product differentiation matrices in $x$ and $y$. Boundary conditions are imposed by replacing the corresponding rows with the appropriate derivative or value conditions.

The resulting dense $2N^2 \times 2N^2$ system is solved by direct factorization (numpy.linalg.solve). For smooth solutions on regular domains---precisely the setting of the present problem---spectral methods achieve exponential convergence as $N$ increases, meaning the error decreases as $O(e^{-cN})$ for some constant $c > 0$. This is the fastest convergence rate among polynomial-based methods, though the dense matrix structure limits the maximum practical $N$ to a few hundred.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)}\label{sec:pinn}

The PINN approach~\citep{raissi2019physics} represents each flux component as a feedforward neural network with shared hidden layers. We use a fully connected architecture with 2 input neurons ($(x, y)$), 3 hidden layers of 64 neurons each with $\tanh$ activation functions, and 2 output neurons ($(\hat{\phi}_1, \hat{\phi}_2)$), yielding 8,706 trainable parameters. The $\tanh$ activation is chosen for its smoothness (infinite differentiability), which facilitates the computation of PDE residuals through automatic differentiation.

The loss function is a weighted sum of PDE and boundary condition residuals:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pinn_loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{N_{\text{int}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{int}}} \sum_{g=1}^{2} |R_g(x_i, y_i)|^2}_{\text{PDE loss}} + \lambda_{\text{BC}} \underbrace{\frac{1}{N_{\text{BC}}} \sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{BC}}} \sum_{g=1}^{2} |B_g(x_j, y_j)|^2}_{\text{BC loss}},
\end{equation}
where $R_g$ denotes the PDE residual at interior collocation points, $B_g$ denotes the boundary condition residual at boundary points, $N_{\text{int}} = 2{,}000$ interior points sampled uniformly at random, $N_{\text{BC}} = 400$ boundary points (100 per side, uniformly spaced), and $\lambda_{\text{BC}} = 1.0$ is the boundary loss weight. All spatial derivatives in $R_g$ and $B_g$ are computed via PyTorch's automatic differentiation.

Training uses the Adam optimizer~\citep{raissi2019physics} for 5,000 iterations with an initial learning rate of $10^{-3}$. No data from analytical or numerical solutions is provided; the network is trained purely from the physics encoded in the loss function. This represents the standard PINN approach without data augmentation, curriculum learning, or adaptive collocation strategies.

\paragraph{Limitations of the PINN approach.}
The PINN faces several challenges specific to this problem: (i)~the coupled multi-group structure introduces competing loss terms that can lead to imbalanced training, (ii)~the Neumann boundary conditions require derivative evaluation at the boundary, which is less stable than Dirichlet enforcement, and (iii)~the smooth $\cosh$-like solution structure may be difficult to approximate efficiently with $\tanh$ basis functions. We include the PINN as a representative of the emerging AI-for-science solver paradigm, not as an optimized competitor; the purpose is to establish the accuracy gap that the analytical solution helps quantify.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Software and hardware.}
All methods are implemented in Python 3.11 using NumPy 1.26, SciPy 1.12, and Matplotlib 3.8 for visualization. The PINN solver uses PyTorch 2.1 with CPU-only execution (no GPU acceleration). All experiments are run on a single CPU core (Apple M-series, ARM64 architecture) for fair timing comparisons. Random seeds are fixed for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Residual evaluation protocol.}
PDE residuals are computed by substituting the computed solution back into the governing equations~\eqref{eq:pde1}--\eqref{eq:pde2} and evaluating the absolute residual. For the analytical solution, derivatives are computed from the closed-form expressions (differentiation of $\cosh$ and $\cos$ terms). For the numerical methods, derivatives are approximated using the same discretization scheme (FDM stencils for FDM, spectral differentiation matrices for spectral, automatic differentiation for PINN, and finite-difference approximation of the FEM interpolant).

Boundary condition residuals are computed at boundary test points by comparing the computed normal derivative (or current) against the prescribed boundary data. Four interior test points---$(0,0)$, $(0.2, 0.2)$, $(-0.2, -0.3)$, and $(0.4, -0.4)$---are used for PDE residual evaluation, along with 16 boundary points distributed across all four sides.

\paragraph{Timing protocol.}
Solve time measures the wall-clock time for the core solution computation, excluding setup, I/O, and post-processing. For the analytical method, this includes mode computation and evaluation at test points. For FDM, FEM, and spectral methods, this includes assembly and direct solve. For the PINN, this includes the full 5,000-iteration training loop.

\section{AI-Driven Numerical Solver Evolution via Famou}\label{sec:famou}

The analytical solution derived in Sections~\ref{sec:analytical} and~\ref{sec:experiments} provides an exact ground truth that enables a novel evaluation paradigm: using the analytical solution as an oracle to drive AI-based solver evolution. We demonstrate this using the Famou framework~\citep{famou2024}, an LLM-based evolutionary optimization system that iteratively refines a numerical solver program guided by evaluator feedback.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We construct a Famou experiment where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Initial program (seed)}: An FDM $101\times101$ solver achieving combined\_score = 0.071.
\item \textbf{Evaluator}: An embedded analytical solver (this paper's method, $N=200$) provides exact flux values at 4 test points, plus PDE and BC residual evaluation. The combined score is $\text{accuracy} \times \text{PDE-factor} \times \text{BC-factor}$, where $\text{accuracy} = 1/(1 + 100 \cdot \bar{\varepsilon}_{\text{rel}})$.
\item \textbf{Evolution}: 30 iterations with population size 10 and 2 islands.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results and Discussion}

After 30 evolution iterations, the best achieved combined\_score remains at 0.07 (seed level), with the evolved solver achieving mean relative error $6\times10^{-4}$ but max PDE residual 19.85. The evolution struggled to improve upon the FDM seed, primarily because:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
\item The evaluator rewards both pointwise accuracy \textit{and} PDE/BC residuals, and the LLM-generated variants tended to optimize one metric at the expense of the other.
\item FDM already provides near-optimal accuracy for its discretization level; further improvement requires fundamentally different algorithmic approaches (higher-order schemes, adaptive refinement, or spectral methods).
\end{enumerate}

This result highlights an important finding: \textit{the analytical solution serves not just as a validation reference, but as a stringent evaluator oracle that exposes the limitations of AI-generated solvers}. The target combined\_score $> 0.95$ (requiring relative error $< 0.5\%$ and PDE residual $< 10^{-6}$) would require either the analytical solution itself or a well-implemented spectral solver---solutions that are qualitatively different from FDM improvements and thus difficult for LLM-based evolution to discover without guidance.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Analytical Methods for Neutron Diffusion}

The analytical treatment of multi-group neutron diffusion has a long history rooted in the foundational textbooks of \citet{duderstadt1976nuclear}, \citet{stacey2007nuclear}, and \citet{lamarsh1966introduction}. The standard separation-of-variables approach applies to bare homogeneous reactors with simple geometries (slab, cylinder, sphere) and homogeneous boundary conditions, yielding the classical bare-reactor flux distributions and criticality conditions.

For more complex configurations, several advanced analytical techniques have been developed. The Laplace Transform provides exact solutions for 1D slab problems with multiple material regions~\citep{lemos2008analytical}. The Finite Fourier Transform, combined with iterative power methods, extends to multigroup eigenvalue problems in 1D~\citep{zanette2021modified} and 2D~\citep{zanette2018modified}, though the solution is obtained as a convergent iteration rather than in closed form. The Residual Power Series Method~\citep{elajou2019analytical} avoids discretization but produces truncated series solutions. Fourier expansion methods for lattice cells~\citep{dion2013fourier} handle rectangular geometries with reflective boundaries.

Nodal methods with analytical basis functions represent a semi-analytical approach. The ANDES solver~\citep{lozano2008andes} uses the ACMFD method to derive coarse-mesh discretization from analytical 1D solutions within each node. Classical nodal methods~\citep{smith1983nodal} exploit transverse integration for efficient 3D calculations.

The Helmholtz equation---the scalar single-group analogue of the diffusion equation---admits well-known cosine-eigenfunction solutions on rectangular domains with Neumann boundary conditions. Our Fourier--eigendecomposition method generalizes this classical construction to the coupled multi-group setting by introducing matrix eigendecomposition as the group-decoupling step. This combination has not been documented in the analytical neutron diffusion literature, and we believe it represents a methodological contribution beyond the specific problem instance solved here.

\paragraph{Generalized Integral Transform Technique (GITT).}
The Generalized Integral Transform Technique (GITT)~\citep{cotta1993integral} is the most directly related prior methodology to our approach. GITT transforms the PDE into a coupled ODE system by expanding the solution in eigenfunctions of an associated eigenvalue problem, then inverting the transform. For rectangular domains with standard BCs, GITT has been applied to diffusion equations~\citep{cotta1993integral}, heat transfer problems, and one-dimensional multi-group neutron diffusion~\citep{bodmann2007neutron}. The key distinction from the present work is that GITT typically solves the resulting ODE system numerically (via truncated eigenfunction expansion and Gauss-Seidel iteration), while our method exploits the algebraic structure of the two-group coupling matrix to obtain a fully closed-form solution via eigendecomposition. Furthermore, GITT for two-dimensional multi-group problems with inhomogeneous Neumann BCs requires handling the inhomogeneous boundary as a distributed source, which introduces additional eigenvalue problems; our direct Fourier matching approach avoids this complication.

\subsection{Classical Numerical Methods}

Finite difference methods for neutron diffusion are well established as reference solvers in both research and production settings~\citep{han2009computational}. Their simplicity and guaranteed convergence rates make them the standard first choice for benchmarking. Finite element methods bring additional flexibility through unstructured meshes and natural Neumann condition handling~\citep{cho2016sensitivity}. Spectral methods, including the Spectral Green's Function approach~\citep{cabraldasilva2023sgf}, achieve exponential convergence for smooth problems on regular domains but are limited to simple geometries.

All of these methods introduce discretization error that fundamentally limits their utility as benchmarks. Even on very fine grids ($201 \times 201$ for FDM, corresponding to $h = 0.005$), the truncation error is $O(10^{-5})$---nine orders of magnitude above machine precision. This error floor means that numerical references cannot distinguish between methods that differ by less than $O(10^{-5})$ in their solution accuracy.

\subsection{Neural PDE Solvers}

Physics-informed neural networks~\citep{raissi2019physics} have generated considerable excitement in the computational physics community by offering a mesh-free, data-lean approach to PDE solution. Applications to neutron diffusion have progressed rapidly: data-enhanced training for eigenvalue problems~\citep{yang2023depinn}, conservative interface conditions~\citep{liu2023physics}, multi-group decoupling loss functions~\citep{huang2024decoupling}, and improved architectures with residual adaptive resampling~\citep{zhang2026r2pinn}.

Neural operators---FNO~\citep{li2021fourier} and DeepONet~\citep{lu2021deeponet}---represent a complementary paradigm that learns solution operators rather than individual solutions, enabling zero-shot generalization across parameter families. The comprehensive comparison by \citet{lu2022comprehensive} demonstrated that FNO excels on regular grids with smooth solutions while DeepONet is more robust to noise and flexible with geometry. Physics-informed extensions~\citep{wang2021learning} combine these approaches with PDE constraints.

\subsection{The Case for Exact Benchmarks}

All neural PDE solvers for neutron diffusion reported in the literature to date are validated against numerical references---typically FDM or FEM solutions on fine grids. This creates two problems for rigorous validation. First, the numerical reference carries its own discretization error, which is often comparable to or larger than the neural solver error being measured. Second, the comparison conflates two sources of error (numerical reference error and neural solver error) into a single observed discrepancy, making it impossible to isolate the contribution of each.

The analytical solution derived in this work eliminates both problems. Its residual is bounded by machine epsilon ($\sim 10^{-16}$), which is 11--13 orders of magnitude below any neural solver accuracy reported in the literature. Any discrepancy between a neural solver and the analytical reference is, to within floating-point precision, entirely attributable to the neural solver.

We argue that the development of exact benchmarks should accompany the development of increasingly sophisticated neural PDE solvers. The Fourier--eigendecomposition technique demonstrated here can be extended to other coupled elliptic systems with compatible boundary conditions, providing a family of exact benchmarks spanning different problem complexities, coupling strengths, and spatial dimensions.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have derived the first closed-form analytical solution for the two-dimensional, two-group steady-state neutron diffusion equation with inhomogeneous Neumann boundary conditions on a rectangular domain. The solution is constructed via Fourier--cosine expansion in the $y$-direction combined with eigendecomposition of the group-coupling matrix $\mat{D}^{-1}\mat{A}$, yielding independent hyperbolic ODEs in $x$ whose coefficients are determined algebraically from the boundary data. The entire procedure is non-iterative, non-discretized (beyond Fourier truncation), and produces a closed-form expression that can be evaluated at arbitrary spatial points to any desired floating-point precision.

Benchmarking against four numerical and data-driven solvers demonstrates the value of an exact reference. The analytical solution achieves PDE residuals at the level of machine epsilon ($\num{2.81e-16}$), outperforming the best numerical method (FEM, $\num{9.20e-7}$) by 10 orders of magnitude and the PINN ($\num{9.54e-3}$) by 13 orders. The FDM convergence study confirms the expected $O(h^2)$ rate, independently validating both the FDM implementation and the analytical reference. The accuracy--efficiency comparison reveals that the analytical solver is not only the most accurate but also the fastest, requiring \num{0.017}\,s compared to \num{98.6}\,s for the PINN.

The analytical solution serves three roles going forward. First, as a \textbf{verification benchmark}: it provides an unambiguous ground truth for testing numerical PDE solvers, with residuals bounded by machine epsilon rather than discretization error. Second, as a \textbf{training reference}: neural PDE solvers that incorporate supervised loss terms can use the analytical solution to generate arbitrarily large and precise training datasets at arbitrary spatial resolution. Third, as a \textbf{methodological template}: the Fourier--eigendecomposition technique demonstrated here is not specific to neutron diffusion and can be applied to any coupled elliptic PDE system on rectangular domains where at least one pair of opposing boundaries admits a Fourier eigenfunction expansion.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

The analytical method developed in this work has several structural limitations that define the scope of its applicability:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Rectangular geometry}: The Fourier--cosine expansion requires two opposing boundaries with homogeneous Neumann conditions. The method does not extend directly to L-shaped domains, cylindrical geometries, or domains with curved boundaries.

\item \textbf{Homogeneous medium}: The diffusion coefficients $D_g$ and cross-sections $\Sigma$ must be spatially uniform throughout the domain. Heterogeneous media (fuel assemblies, control rods) require different techniques such as interface-matching conditions or nodal decomposition.

\item \textbf{BC structure}: The method requires at least two opposing faces with homogeneous Neumann or homogeneous Dirichlet conditions to serve as the Fourier expansion direction. Arbitrary mixed BCs on all four faces cannot be handled without additional particular-solution construction.

\item \textbf{Fixed-source problem}: The formulation treats the system as a fixed-source problem (given boundary current drives the solution). Extension to the $k$-eigenvalue (criticality) problem would require iteration over the fission source term.

\item \textbf{Two-group truncation}: While the eigendecomposition principle generalizes to $G$ groups (yielding a $G\times G$ eigendecomposition problem), computational cost and numerical stability may degrade for large $G$ due to the increasing condition number of the coefficient matrix $\mat{Q}_n$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future work.}
Three extensions merit investigation. First, the technique can be generalized to \textbf{heterogeneous media} (piecewise-constant cross-sections) by treating material interfaces as internal boundaries with continuity conditions for both flux and current. The Fourier expansion in $y$ remains valid, but the $x$-direction solution involves matching conditions at each interface, expanding the algebraic system. Second, extension to \textbf{three spatial dimensions} is straightforward: the $z$-direction admits another Fourier expansion (cosine for Neumann, sine for Dirichlet), producing a doubly indexed family of 1D ODEs that are decoupled by the same eigenvector matrix $\mat{P}$. The computational cost grows as $O(N_y \cdot N_z)$ but remains polynomial. Third, the \textbf{eigenvalue (criticality) problem}, where the fission source is scaled by $1/k_{\text{eff}}$, can be addressed by parametrizing the analytical solution over $k_{\text{eff}}$ and applying a bisection or Newton search to find the value that satisfies the criticality condition (non-trivial solution with finite norm).
